## 1. CAURD Legal History — The Operator Uncertainty Problem

### The Gap
The Conditional Adult-Use Retail Dispensary (CAURD) program launched in late 2022 as New York's first adult-use retail licensing track. It was challenged by companies that had submitted applications for standard retail licenses, arguing CAURD created an unconstitutional preference. Courts issued and lifted injunctions multiple times in 2023–2024.

### The Drift
CAURD operators who received licenses and built out locations during the injunction periods operated in legal uncertainty — their licenses were valid but their operations could be challenged. By 2026, most CAURD licenses are operational with litigation resolved or settled, but the history creates documentation obligations.

### The Posture
CAURD operators should maintain complete documentation of their original CAURD application, qualification evidence, and any court or OCM correspondence related to the litigation period. This documentation supports the license's legitimacy in any future licensing audit.

## 2. Municipal Opt-Out — The 1,000+ Municipality Map

### The Gap
N.Y. Cannabis Law § 131 allowed municipalities to opt out of adult-use retail dispensaries by December 31, 2021. Over 1,000 municipalities opted out. Subsequent legislation has modified the opt-out and opt-in process. The map of opted-in vs. opted-out municipalities changes as communities reconsider their positions.

### The Drift
An operator who received a license for a location in a municipality that was opted-in at application time may face complications if that municipality's opt-in status changes. The legal framework for existing operations in a newly opted-out municipality is not fully resolved.

### The Exposure
Operators who have not verified current opt-in/opt-out status for their operating municipality before applying for or expanding a license face the risk of a license issued for an ineligible location.

### The Posture
Verify current municipal opt-in status through the OCM's official database before every licensing action. The database is the authoritative source — local government websites and news reports may be outdated.

## 3. Legacy Registered Organization Transition — Metrc Migration Artifacts

### The Gap
New York's legacy medical marijuana Registered Organizations (ROs) operated under a prior tracking system before Metrc was implemented. The migration to Metrc in 2023 created data quality issues — lot histories, transfer records, and inventory balances carried over (or not) from the prior system.

### The Drift
OCM inspections of legacy RO operators in 2024–2025 have found Metrc inventory discrepancies that trace to migration-era data quality failures — records from the pre-Metrc system that were incorrectly migrated or never migrated. These appear as large, unexplained inventory variances in Metrc.

### The Posture
If you are a legacy RO operator, audit your Metrc migration records specifically. Identify any lot or inventory record that traces to the migration period. Reconcile migration-era discrepancies proactively — before OCM inspection.

## 4. On-Site Consumption — Rules Still Developing

### The Gap
N.Y. Cannabis Law § 63 authorizes on-site consumption sites. As of early 2026, the OCM's rules for on-site consumption are still being finalized. Operators who have received on-site consumption licenses or who are planning applications are building operational models against an incomplete rule set.

### The Drift
The OCM has issued interim guidance on on-site consumption operations, but final rules are pending. The interim guidance creates compliance uncertainty — operators who build to interim guidance standards may need to modify operations when final rules are published.

### The Posture
Build on-site consumption operations with the highest defensible standard against interim guidance. Document that your operations comply with interim guidance. Be prepared to adapt when final rules publish.

---

## 5. OCM Resource Constraints — Enforcement Unevenness

### The Gap
The OCM launched significantly under-resourced relative to the scale of New York's cannabis market. Inspection capacity has grown through 2023–2025 but remains thinner than the licensee base would require for consistent enforcement coverage.

### The Drift
OCM enforcement has been concentrated in New York City, where the retail market is densest and the unlicensed market competition is most visible. Upstate and rural operators have experienced fewer OCM inspections per year than their New York City counterparts.

### The Posture
Resource-constrained enforcement is not an excuse for compliance shortcuts. The operators who build robust compliance programs regardless of inspection frequency are the ones positioned for long-term survival. OCM enforcement intensity will increase as resources grow.
