## 1. The Unlicensed Market — The Compliance Environment's Shadow

**The OCM expectation:** Licensed operators sell legal product; unlicensed operations are enforced against.

**The floor reality:** New York's unlicensed cannabis market is the largest in the country by most estimates. The delays in adult-use licensing created a window in which unlicensed storefronts (sometimes called "smoke shops" or "bodegas") proliferated. Licensed operators in New York are competing against unlicensed product sold at lower prices without the compliance overhead.

**What we're seeing (2023–2026):**
- Licensed CAURD operators in New York City competing directly against unlicensed operations on the same blocks
- OCM and NYPD enforcement sweeps against unlicensed operations — but the scale of enforcement has not matched the scale of the unlicensed market
- Licensed operators building "we're the legal option" messaging into their retail positioning
- Some licensed retail operators documenting unlicensed competitor locations and reporting to OCM as a market protection strategy

---

## 2. CAURD Build-Outs During the Injunction Period

**The OCM expectation:** CAURD licensees operate under their license conditions.

**The floor reality:** CAURD operators who built out locations during the injunction periods — when their licenses were valid but court orders created legal uncertainty — made capital investments in an ambiguous environment. The injunction periods also delayed OCM's ability to issue additional licenses, creating a market where CAURD operators had limited or no licensed competition.

**What we're seeing (2023–2026):**
- CAURD operators who opened during injunction periods and are now competing with a growing AURD (standard adult-use retail) licensee base
- Build-out cost recovery challenges for CAURD operators who spent heavily on locations that now face increased competition
- OCM compliance support for CAURD operators has been inconsistent — some CAURD operators report difficulty getting OCM guidance on specific operational questions

---

## 3. Metrc Launch Growing Pains

**The OCM expectation:** Accurate Metrc tracking from day one of adult-use.

**The floor reality:** New York's Metrc implementation in 2023 was one of the most complex state Metrc launches in the system's history — incorporating legacy RO tracking data, CAURD retail launches, and new cultivator/processor onboarding simultaneously.

**What we're seeing (2023–2026):**
- Legacy RO operators with Metrc inventory discrepancies traced to migration-era data errors that have never been formally reconciled with OCM
- New CAURD retailers discovering Metrc receipt errors when they receive product from new cultivators/processors who are still learning the system
- OCM Metrc support response times that have been slower than the market needs — creating backlogs of unresolved Metrc support tickets
- Operators building internal Metrc troubleshooting capacity rather than relying on OCM response

---

## 4. 90-Day Retention — The OCM Standard

**The OCM expectation:** 90-day camera footage retention at all licensed premises.

**The floor reality:** New York's 90-day retention requirement applies across all license types. For CAURD retailers who built out locations in 2022–2023, the security systems installed at launch are now 2–3 years old but were often configured for minimum retention. The same storage calculation problem as other 90-day states applies.

**What we're seeing (2023–2026):**
- OCM security reviews requesting footage from Day 85 as a standard test
- CAURD operators whose security vendors provided "compliant" systems but didn't specifically address 90-day storage at the planned camera count
- High-camera-count retail dispensaries (Manhattan flagships with 20+ cameras) discovering storage capacity gaps that their vendors didn't flag at installation

---

## 5. Municipal Opt-In/Opt-Out Volatility

**The OCM expectation:** Dispensaries operate in opted-in municipalities.

**The floor reality:** New York's municipality map is the most complex in the country — over 1,000 municipalities opted out, but the list has been evolving as communities reconsider. The opt-in/opt-out status of a specific municipality changes through local legislative action, and the OCM database is not always current with local action.

**What we're seeing (2023–2026):**
- Operators who relied on local government representations about opt-in status discovering the OCM's official record differed
- New York City neighborhood-level variations (community boards, not the official opt-out framework) creating de facto local pressure on dispensary locations
- Successful opt-in campaigns by industry advocates in previously opted-out markets opening new viable locations
