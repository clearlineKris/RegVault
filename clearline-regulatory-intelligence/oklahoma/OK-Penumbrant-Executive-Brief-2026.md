## Executive Summary

Oklahoma's medical marijuana market has undergone a dramatic consolidation. The OMMA, with a smaller licensee base to oversee, is applying more intensive scrutiny per operator. The compliance risk profile in 2026 is concentrated around acquisition-driven compliance gaps, pesticide residue findings from the price-pressure era, and the dual-recording requirement that trips up high-volume dispensaries daily.

---

## Priority Risk Domain 1: Acquisition Compliance Due Diligence

**Risk Level:** 🔴 High — Structural  
**Trigger:** OMMA corrective action inheritance in license transfers

The consolidation wave has created operators holding 3–10+ licenses. Each acquired license carries its compliance history. OMMA corrective actions issued against a license before acquisition follow the license — not the prior operator.

**Executive Decision Points:**
- Has a compliance audit been conducted for each acquired license?
- Are there any outstanding corrective action plans on acquired licenses?
- Have Metrc accounts for acquired licenses been reviewed for systemic discrepancy patterns?

**Recommended Posture:** Build compliance due diligence into every acquisition process before close. Outstanding CAPs and Metrc pattern anomalies should be priced into the deal or resolved as a closing condition.

---

## Priority Risk Domain 2: Patient Registry / Metrc Dual-Recording

**Risk Level:** 🔴 High — Operational (Dispensaries)  
**Trigger:** OAC 310:681-3-2; OAC 310:681-7; OMMA inspection pattern

The dual-recording requirement is a daily compliance friction point. Timestamp discrepancies between Metrc and patient registry are being treated as diversion indicators. High-volume dispensaries with manual dual-entry workflows face the highest exposure.

**Executive Decision Points:**
- Does your POS system integrate Metrc and patient registry recording in a single transaction?
- Is there a defined operational protocol for ensuring registry currency during high-volume periods?
- Have you reviewed your registry/Metrc alignment against the last 30 days of sales?

**Recommended Posture:** POS integration is the compliance solution. Manual two-entry workflows are a compliance liability at any significant volume.

---

## Priority Risk Domain 3: Pesticide Residue from Price-Pressure Era

**Risk Level:** 🟠 High  
**Trigger:** OAC 310:681-5-5; OMMA/ODAFF coordination

The IPM corner-cutting of 2022–2024 is producing test failures in 2025–2026. The secondary risk: supply-chain contamination from acquired facilities or purchased biomass.

**Executive Decision Points:**
- Is pre-harvest pesticide residue testing in your current protocol?
- Have acquired cultivation facilities had their pesticide application logs audited?
- Are all active pesticide applicators verified for current ODAFF credentials?

**Recommended Posture:** Pre-harvest testing is the only early-warning system. Treat any pesticide residue finding in testing as a systemic problem to investigate, not an isolated event to remediate.

---

## Priority Risk Domain 4: Security System Currency

**Risk Level:** 🟠 High — Infrastructure  
**Trigger:** OAC 310:681-2-10; aging install base

Oklahoma's large cohort of 2019–2021 security installations is aging without systematic maintenance. 60-day retention gaps, hard drive failures, and coverage gaps from unreported facility changes are the primary findings.

**Executive Decision Points:**
- Have all security systems (including acquired facilities) been audited for 60-day retention compliance?
- Are alarm monitoring contracts current and verified?
- Has coverage been updated to reflect any facility modifications since installation?

**Recommended Posture:** Run a security infrastructure audit across your entire portfolio. For acquired facilities, assume the security system needs a full audit until proven otherwise.
