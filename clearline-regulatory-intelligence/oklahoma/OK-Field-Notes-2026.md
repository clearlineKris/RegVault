## 1. The Consolidation Compliance Problem

**The OMMA expectation:** License holders comply with OAC 310:681 from first day of licensure.

**The floor reality:** Oklahoma's rapid license issuance created a market where many operators launched without mature compliance infrastructure. The consolidation wave of 2023–2025 concentrated licenses in fewer hands — but many of those acquisitions were distress buys where compliance due diligence was secondary to speed and price.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Operators holding 3–8 licenses acquired through consolidation with mixed compliance histories across their portfolio
- OMMA corrective actions inherited through acquisition — the buyer becomes responsible for the prior licensee's outstanding CAP requirements
- Metrc accounts with years of inconsistent tagging practices that auditors flag as systemic issues rather than isolated discrepancies
- Security systems from 2020–2021 installations that have never been serviced or audited for retention compliance

---

## 2. Patient Registry Dual-Recording — Daily Operations Friction

**The OMMA expectation:** Every sale recorded in both Metrc and the patient registry simultaneously.

**The floor reality:** Oklahoma's two-system recording requirement is a daily operational friction point at every dispensary. POS systems that handle both simultaneously are more reliable; manual two-entry workflows break down under volume pressure.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Busy dispensaries (especially during promotional periods) accumulating patient registry entry backlogs of 30–90 minutes behind Metrc entries — generating mismatch findings when OMMA compares timestamps
- OMMA treating timestamp discrepancies between Metrc and registry as potential diversion red flags, not as operational friction
- Dispensary operators who upgraded POS systems to integrated solutions finding OMMA inspection outcomes significantly improved
- Some dispensaries assigning a dedicated "compliance runner" during high-volume periods whose only job is ensuring registry entries are current

---

## 3. The OMMA Inspection Dynamic — Medical-Market Intensity

**The OMMA expectation:** Uniform inspection of all licensed facilities.

**The floor reality:** With a smaller licensee base in 2025–2026 than at peak, OMMA inspectors have relatively more capacity per licensee. Inspection frequency has increased for some operators — particularly those with corrective action histories or who are in markets where OMMA suspects consolidated black-market linkages.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Some Oklahoma operators receiving 4+ OMMA inspections in 12 months — versus 1–2 during the boom-era coverage thinning
- OMMA inspections increasingly including pesticide application log audits alongside Metrc and security reviews
- Inspectors cross-referencing Metrc inventory against physical plant counts — not accepting Metrc as the only verification
- OMMA using third-party data analytics to flag Metrc patterns that suggest diversion — operators with irregular transfer patterns receiving targeted inspections

---

## 4. Pesticide Compliance Under Price Pressure

**The OMMA expectation:** Only EPA-registered, permitted pesticides; full application log; ODAFF credentials for applicators.

**The floor reality:** Oklahoma's oversupplied market drove wholesale prices down sharply. IPM budgets were among the first costs cut. The corner-cutting from 2022–2024 is surfacing in 2025–2026 test results.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Pesticide residue findings in Oklahoma testing data across multiple cultivator operations — particularly imidacloprid (systemic, persistent) and myclobutanil
- Supply-chain contamination from distressed cultivators selling untested biomass to processors without disclosure
- OMMA and ODAFF beginning to coordinate on pesticide findings — a residue finding in testing can trigger an ODAFF investigation of the applicator
- Operators who maintained IPM discipline during the price pressure discovering it as a market differentiator — clean test results as a quality signal to processors

---

## 5. Security System Aging — The 2020 Install Problem

**The OMMA expectation:** Camera coverage current; 60-day retention; alarm monitoring active.

**The floor reality:** Oklahoma's initial licensing rush in 2019–2021 produced a large cohort of security system installations. By 2026, many of these systems are 5–6 years old without maintenance or capacity reviews.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Hard drive failures in DVR/NVR systems installed in 2020 — operators discovering the failure during an OMMA inspection requesting footage from 30 days prior
- Camera resolution sufficient at installation but not at current facility footprint (expansions, layout changes)
- Alarm monitoring contracts lapsed during financial distress — operators on auto-pay who didn't notice the monitoring company changed ownership or ceased service
- OMMA flagging security findings as a secondary concern in distress-driven inspections; operators in financial difficulty tend to have security infrastructure problems as well
