# Ohio Field Notes — 2026 Edition

**Document Type:** Field Notes — On-the-Ground Layer  
**Jurisdiction:** Ohio (OH)  
**Version:** 1.0 — April 2026  
**Rule Basis:** ORC Chapter 3780; Ohio Admin. Code Chapter 3780; ODCC 2025–2026  
**Compiled by:** ClearLine Regulatory Intelligence  

---

> *"Ohio adult-use launched June 7, 2024. The first operators to open were building compliance systems against rules that were still being finalized. Two years in, the floor has stories — and the inspectors have priorities. Here's what we're seeing."*

---

## 1. The Adult-Use Launch Hangover

**The statutory expectation:** Operators licensed under adult-use rules comply from day one.

**The floor reality:** Many Ohio adult-use licensees converted from the existing medical marijuana program. The conversion meant new licenses, new Metrc environments, and new operational requirements landing on businesses that were already running. The compliance infrastructure for adult-use and medical had to operate in parallel from launch day.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Dual-track operators running dual Metrc accounts, dual manifests, and dual compliance calendars simultaneously — and occasionally mixing them up under operational pressure
- Adult-use conversion operators who updated their physical plant but not their compliance training — staff trained on medical Metrc workflows encountering adult-use-specific requirements during inspections
- ODCC inspectors arriving at converted facilities and expecting adult-use compliance from day one with no "transition grace period" despite the operational complexity
- Some early licensees who received both medical and adult-use licenses without a clear ODCC guidance document on dual-track operations

---

## 2. The Separate Transporter License — Daily Operations Impact

**The statutory expectation:** Inter-facility product movement requires a separate ODCC Transporter license.

**The floor reality:** For multi-facility operators (cultivator + processor + dispensary), the Transporter license requirement adds an operational layer that Colorado operators don't face. Every batch of flower moving from the cultivation facility to the processing facility requires a licensed transporter — meaning either an in-house transporter employee holding the license, or a contracted transport company.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Single-facility operators discovering they need the Transporter license when they attempt to move product for testing — testing facilities are off-site by definition in Ohio
- Multi-facility operators building their own internal transport function with a Transporter license holder on staff
- Third-party transporter companies forming specifically to serve the Ohio cannabis market — a new industry segment that didn't exist before adult-use
- Manifest/Metrc mismatch issues when third-party transporters create manifests that don't align with the originating licensee's Metrc entry

## 3. Packaging Violations — The Early Enforcement Signal

**The statutory expectation:** All products meet ORC § 3780.20 and Admin. Code 3780-3-10 labeling requirements.

**The floor reality:** Packaging and labeling compliance is where ODCC has focused early retail inspections. The violations being found are not deliberate noncompliance — they are products that were labeled before the final adult-use label rules were published, products converted from medical labeling, and products where the batch/lot number linkage to Metrc is not clearly visible.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Retail dispensaries receiving product with medical-era labels and selling it as adult-use — creating a dual-track labeling violation
- Batch/lot numbers printed too small or in formats that don't match the Metrc entry — inspectors flagging this as a traceability failure
- Child-resistant packaging passing visual inspection but failing ODCC's standard when the inspector attempts to open it — packages that are "technically compliant" but fail real-world use testing
- Edible products where the "per serving" and "per package" THC notation is inconsistently displayed — triggering citations

## 4. The 90-Day Retention Requirement — A Hidden Infrastructure Cost

**The statutory expectation:** Security footage retained for 90 days minimum (Admin. Code 3780-3-06(B)).

**The floor reality:** Ohio's 90-day retention requirement is one of the strictest in the country — Colorado requires 40 days; Nevada 30. Most commercial cannabis DVR/NVR systems are configured for 30–60 day retention by default. Ohio operators who installed security systems without verifying 90-day retention capacity at their camera count and resolution are non-compliant without knowing it.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Operators discovering their storage capacity falls short of 90 days during their first ODCC security review — not an inspection catch, but a pre-inspection self-audit finding
- NVR systems configured at 1080p (high definition) overwriting at 75 days due to storage capacity — ODCC expects 90; the operator doesn't know the gap exists
- Security vendors marketing "Ohio compliant" systems that meet camera coverage requirements but not storage duration requirements
- ODCC inspectors requesting footage from Day 85–90 as a specific compliance test during inspections — targeting the retention edge

---

## 5. IPM and Pesticide Compliance — The ODA Layer

**The statutory expectation:** EPA-registered pesticides only; ODA-licensed applicator for commercial applications; application log.

**The floor reality:** Ohio's ODA pesticide applicator license requirement adds a compliance dimension not present in all states. Ohio cannabis cultivators who hire IPM contractors are responsible for verifying that the contractor holds a current ODA commercial pesticide applicator license. Contractors who let their license lapse — or who hold a license in a different category — create a compliance gap at the operator level.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- IPM contractors with out-of-state licenses applying to Ohio cannabis facilities — which does not satisfy the ODA requirement
- Operators who didn't know about the ODA applicator license requirement until ODCC asked for their IPM contractor's credentials on inspection
- ODA and ODCC beginning to coordinate on pesticide inspection data — pesticide residue findings in testing are being cross-checked against application logs and contractor credentials
- The same prohibited compounds seen in Colorado (myclobutanil, imidacloprid) appearing in Ohio test results, with similar supply-chain-contamination patterns

---

## 6. ODCC Inspector Dynamics — The New Agency Learning Curve

**The statutory expectation:** ODCC inspectors enforce the rules uniformly.

**The floor reality:** ODCC is a young agency. Many of its inspectors were hired specifically for adult-use cannabis regulation and have varying depth of industry experience. Inspection protocols were refined in real-time during 2024. The 2026 inspection environment is meaningfully more consistent than the 2024 launch environment — but variability persists.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- ODCC inspectors at different regional offices applying the dual-track segregation standard differently
- Inspector "learning inspections" in 2024 — visits that were simultaneously compliance checks and ODCC fact-finding on how Ohio facilities actually operate
- Post-inspection corrective action plans that lack specificity — operators receiving CAPs that don't clearly identify the remediation standard, leading to second CAPs for incomplete remediation
- The industry association (Ohio Cannabis Business Alliance / OCBA) actively communicating with ODCC on inspector consistency — an unusual direct channel that is actively shaping compliance standards
