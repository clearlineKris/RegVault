## The Ohio Pesticide Compliance Structure

Ohio cannabis pesticide compliance operates at the intersection of two regulatory frameworks:

1. **ODCC Framework (Admin. Code 3780-3-12)**: Cannabis-specific pesticide rules — what products are permitted, application log requirements, re-entry intervals
2. **ODA Framework (ORC § 921.02; OAC Chapter 901:5)**: Ohio's broader pesticide law — requires commercial pesticide applicators to hold a current ODA license; applies to cannabis facilities as agricultural operations

The compliance gap: operators who know their ODCC obligations but not their ODA obligations are exposed on the ODA side.

---

## ODCC Pesticide Rule Requirements

**Admin. Code 3780-3-12(A):** Only EPA-registered pesticides permitted. Must be labeled for cannabis, food crops, or designated as non-phytotoxic.

**Admin. Code 3780-3-12(B):** Application log required — every application must document:
- Product name and EPA registration number
- Date and time
- Applicator name and ODA license number
- Application rate and method
- Target pest or disease
- Area of application

**Admin. Code 3780-3-12(C):** 48-hour re-entry interval in canopy areas after application.

**Admin. Code 3780-3-12(D):** IPM plan required — on file with ODCC, updated annually.

## ODA Applicator License Requirement

### What It Is
Ohio's Pesticide Law (ORC § 921.02) requires that commercial pesticide applications — i.e., applications at a business operation — be made by or under the supervision of an ODA-licensed Commercial Pesticide Applicator.

### What It Means for Cannabis Operators
- Every person who applies pesticides at your cannabis facility for commercial purposes (including your employees) must hold or operate under the supervision of an ODA Commercial Pesticide Applicator license
- **Category 24 (Right-of-Way) or food-crop categories** — verify the correct ODA license category applies to your specific application type
- IPM contractors must hold a current ODA Pesticide Business License AND appropriate applicator credentials
- Out-of-state licenses do not satisfy Ohio's ODA requirement

### Enforcement Coordination (2025–2026)
ODA and ODCC are beginning to share inspection data. A pesticide residue finding in ODCC's testing system can trigger an ODA investigation of the applicator. This creates a two-agency enforcement exposure from a single violation.

## Supply Chain Accountability Gap

Ohio mirrors Colorado's supply chain pesticide problem: there is no "innocent purchaser" defense. If a prohibited compound is found in product testing, the licensed cultivator faces the enforcement action — regardless of whether the compound came from their application or a supplier's.

**Operator actions:**
- Require pesticide use disclosure from every clone or plant supplier at point of purchase
- Require same-day application records from IPM contractors — their log is part of your compliance record
- Include pesticide residue testing in your pre-harvest testing protocol

## Compliance Hardening Checklist — Ohio

- [ ] All pesticides on premises verified against ODCC/ODA permitted list
- [ ] Application log current, specific, and includes ODA applicator license number
- [ ] All in-house pesticide applicators hold or operate under ODA Commercial Pesticide Applicator license
- [ ] All IPM contractors verified for current ODA Pesticide Business License + applicator credentials
- [ ] No Eagle 20EW, imidacloprid, or other prohibited compound on premises or in supply chain
- [ ] 48-hour re-entry intervals documented and enforced
- [ ] IPM plan on file with ODCC, current version
- [ ] Supplier pesticide disclosure obtained for all plant/clone purchases
- [ ] Pre-harvest pesticide residue testing in protocol
- [ ] Staff training on prohibited compounds documented
