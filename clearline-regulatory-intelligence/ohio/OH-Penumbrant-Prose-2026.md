## Purpose

This document maps the enforcement gray zones in Ohio's 2026 cannabis regulatory environment. Ohio is a post-transition state — adult-use rules are active but enforcement history is thin, meaning the first enforcement actions are still establishing the de facto standards that will define compliance for years.

## 1. Dual-Track Inventory Segregation — The Medical/Adult-Use Boundary

### The Gap
Ohio Admin. Code 3780-4-02(F) requires dual-license operators to maintain segregated Metrc environments for medical and adult-use inventory. But the rule does not define what "segregation" means physically for shared cultivation facilities — can a single cultivation room house both medical and adult-use plants if they are separately tagged? The ODCC has not issued interpretive guidance.

### The Drift
ODCC enforcement activity in 2024–2025 included several corrective actions involving dual-track inventory discrepancies. The corrective action language has varied — some actions treat commingled physical space as a violation; others treat it as a tracking failure only. No consistent standard has emerged.

### The Exposure
Dual-license cultivators operating shared facilities face inspection risk that depends entirely on which ODCC inspector they draw. The exposure is highest for harvest rooms shared between medical and adult-use crop cycles.

### The Posture
Physical segregation is the safer posture. Designate rooms or at minimum defined rows within rooms as exclusively medical or adult-use. Document the physical segregation plan. Don't rely on Metrc tag differentiation alone as your segregation evidence.

## 2. Transporter License — The Self-Transport Question

### The Gap
ORC § 3780.13 requires a separate ODCC Transporter license for inter-facility movement. The rule is clear for third-party transfers. Less clear: can an operator transport their own product between their own licensed facilities without a Transporter license? The statute uses "person who transports" — not "third-party transporter."

### The Drift
ODCC has informally communicated that a cultivator moving product to their own processing facility requires the Transporter license. But this position has not been published in a bulletin or formal rule interpretation. Two ODCC compliance officers have given different answers to the same question.

### The Exposure
An operator moving their own product between their own licensed Ohio facilities without a Transporter license faces a potential unlicensed activity citation if ODCC takes the broad interpretation.

### The Posture
Obtain the Transporter license if you are moving product between any of your licensed facilities — even self-owned. The license fee is modest compared to an unlicensed activity finding.

## 3. Home Cultivation Leakage — The Commercial-to-Home Boundary

### The Gap
ORC § 3780.34 permits home cultivation of up to 6 plants. The statute prohibits home-grown cannabis from being sold, transferred for value, or commercially processed. But the boundary between a personal home grower and an unlicensed commercial operation is not defined by plant count, potency, or production method — it is defined by intent and activity.

### The Drift
ODCC has not published enforcement guidance on what activity converts a home grow into unlicensed commercial cultivation. The 2025 enforcement record includes unlicensed cultivation actions, but the facts involve obviously commercial-scale operations. The middle ground — large personal home grows with informal sharing — has not been tested.

### The Exposure
The home cultivation provision creates a de facto unlicensed market adjacent to the regulated market. Licensed operators who see unlicensed product competing in their market have limited recourse; ODCC's enforcement resources are focused on licensed facility compliance, not black-market enforcement.

### The Posture
For licensed operators: this is a market observation, not a compliance action. For operators with employees who home-grow: ensure no home-grown product ever enters your licensed facility. The commingling of home-grown and licensed product is a traceable compliance violation.

## 4. Level I–V Dispensary Tiers — The Volume Ambiguity

### The Gap
Ohio's dispensary licensing uses a Level I–V tiered structure (ORC § 3780.12). The rules define tiers by application criteria and fees, but do not clearly define the operational differences between tiers in terms of daily purchase limits customers can buy, annual sales volume expectations, or whether a lower-tier license faces stricter proximity or zoning restrictions.

### The Drift
Municipalities have interpreted the tier structure differently for zoning purposes — some treating Level I as a "neighborhood dispensary" compatible with residential proximity; others applying the same restrictions regardless of tier. ODCC has not standardized municipal zoning guidance for the tier structure.

### The Exposure
A Level I dispensary that grows its customer base to Level IV or V volume is operating above its license tier's intended scope without having upgraded. The upgrade pathway (and whether retroactive compliance is required) is unclear.

### The Posture
If your dispensary is operating at volumes that may exceed your license tier's intended scope, proactively contact ODCC to discuss tier review. Document the inquiry. Don't wait for enforcement to define the boundary.

## 5. Testing Lab Independence — The Emerging Conflict Question

### The Gap
Ohio Admin. Code 3780-5 requires testing laboratory independence — no financial relationship with clients. But "financial relationship" is not defined. Does a testing lab that provides volume discounts to a major client have a financial relationship that compromises independence? Does a lab that receives referral fees from a consultant who also serves cannabis operators have a conflict?

### The Drift
Ohio's testing lab independence requirement mirrors national trends but Ohio-specific enforcement is minimal in 2024–2025. The first lab independence action in Ohio will define the standard for the market.

### The Posture
Engage testing labs only with written agreements that explicitly prohibit any financial arrangement beyond the per-test service fee. If your lab offers volume discounts, document that the discount is uniformly applied and not specific to your business.

## 6. ODCC Enforcement Trajectory — What the First Two Years Signal

### The Gap
Ohio's adult-use enforcement history is short. The ODCC issued its first adult-use corrective actions in Q4 2024. Two years of enforcement data is insufficient to map clear patterns.

### The Drift
What can be read from early enforcement:
- ODCC has prioritized packaging and labeling violations in early retail inspections
- Metrc tracking discrepancies are generating corrective actions, not just warnings
- Security findings (camera coverage, retention) are appearing in corrective action plans
- Dual-track inventory segregation violations are a growing category

### The Posture
Ohio operators in 2026 are building compliance programs against an enforcement environment that is still crystallizing. The operators who build to the highest defensible standard now — not the minimum — will be positioned well as enforcement matures.
