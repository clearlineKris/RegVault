## Executive Summary

Ohio's adult-use cannabis market is two years old. The Ohio Division of Cannabis Control (ODCC) has moved from a launch-mode agency to an active enforcement posture. The 2026 compliance environment reflects the accumulated learning from two years of inspections, corrective actions, and operational feedback — and the emerging patterns are clear enough to plan against.

---

## Priority Risk Domain 1: Dual-Track Inventory Segregation

**Risk Level:** 🔴 High  
**Trigger:** Ohio Admin. Code 3780-4-02(F); corrective action patterns 2024–2025

Ohio's dual medical/adult-use licensing structure creates a compliance burden that single-track states don't face. Operators holding both medical and adult-use licenses must maintain segregated Metrc environments and — ODCC's de facto standard — physically segregated inventory. The enforcement record from 2024–2025 shows dual-track segregation is a priority inspection target.

**Executive Decision Points:**
- Are your medical and adult-use cultivation areas physically designated and documented?
- Are your Metrc accounts for medical and adult-use separately managed with no cross-entries?
- Have you trained staff specifically on the dual-track compliance distinction?

**Recommended Posture:** Physical segregation, not just digital tagging, is the defensible standard. If you are operating shared cultivation space, designate zones and document the designation formally.

## Priority Risk Domain 2: 90-Day Camera Retention — Infrastructure Gap

**Risk Level:** 🔴 High — Infrastructure  
**Trigger:** Ohio Admin. Code 3780-3-06(B)

Ohio's 90-day video retention requirement is one of the strictest in the country. Most cannabis security systems are configured for 30–60 days by default. Operators who installed security infrastructure without explicitly verifying 90-day capacity at their camera count and resolution are likely non-compliant without knowing it.

**Executive Decision Points:**
- Has your security vendor confirmed your DVR/NVR system retains footage for 90 days at your current camera count and resolution?
- Have you physically tested footage retrieval from Day 85–90?
- Does your system have adequate storage buffer to accommodate your full operational footprint?

**Recommended Posture:** This is an infrastructure test, not a policy question. Run the test. Pull footage from Day 88. If you can't, your system is non-compliant. The fix is storage, not policy.

## Priority Risk Domain 3: Packaging & Labeling — Early Enforcement Focus

**Risk Level:** 🟠 High  
**Trigger:** Ohio Admin. Code 3780-3-10; ODCC early retail inspection pattern

ODCC's early retail enforcement has concentrated on packaging and labeling. The violations being found are not intentional — they are products transitioning from medical-era labels, batch/lot numbers not matching Metrc format, and per-serving THC notation errors. But good-faith intent does not prevent a corrective action.

**Executive Decision Points:**
- Have all product labels been audited against the current Admin. Code 3780-3-10 requirements?
- Are all batch/lot numbers on retail products linked to a verifiable Metrc entry?
- Have medical-era labeled products been removed from your adult-use inventory?

**Recommended Posture:** Conduct a full label audit against the 2026 rule standard. Pull any product with medical-era labeling from adult-use inventory. Implement a label QA checkpoint before any new product reaches the retail floor.

## Priority Risk Domain 4: Transporter License — Operational Continuity

**Risk Level:** 🟠 High — Structural  
**Trigger:** ORC § 3780.13

Ohio's mandatory Transporter license for inter-facility movement creates an operational dependency that can become a continuity risk. If your transporter (internal or contracted) loses their license, product movement stops until the license is restored.

**Executive Decision Points:**
- Do you hold an ODCC Transporter license for in-house transport, or are you dependent on a contracted transporter?
- If contracted, what is your contingency plan if the transporter relationship is interrupted?
- Is every inter-facility movement — including to testing labs — covered by a licensed transporter?

**Recommended Posture:** If you operate multiple facilities, holding your own Transporter license is a resilience investment. Contracted transporters should have verified license status confirmed quarterly.

---

## Priority Risk Domain 5: Pesticide Compliance + ODA Applicator Layer

**Risk Level:** 🟠 High  
**Trigger:** Ohio Admin. Code 3780-3-12; ORC § 921.02 (ODA pesticide law)

Ohio's dual pesticide compliance obligation — ODCC application rules AND ODA licensed applicator requirement — creates a two-agency risk surface. ODA and ODCC are beginning to coordinate on pesticide findings. A pesticide residue finding in testing can trigger both an ODCC corrective action and an ODA investigation of your applicator.

**Executive Decision Points:**
- Has every pesticide applicator working in your facility (including IPM contractors) been verified for a current ODA commercial applicator license?
- Is your pesticide application log specific enough to support an ODA investigation if needed?
- Are you conducting pre-harvest pesticide residue testing?

**Recommended Posture:** Verify ODA license status for every applicator now. Build verification into your IPM contractor onboarding. Pre-harvest testing is the only way to know about supply-chain contamination before it becomes a quarantine event.
