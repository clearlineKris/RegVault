## 1. METRC on the MIP Floor

**The statutory expectation:** Same-day batch entries. Clean transfer manifests. Real-time inventory accuracy.

**The floor reality:** MIP operations — especially extraction and infusion lines running overnight shifts — routinely produce batch completion times that don't align with the METRC entry window. Trim shift supervisors who are also responsible for METRC entry often complete their final entry after the batch completes but before the first METRC login window of the morning.

The problem isn't noncompliance — it's documentation timing. Facilities that run their METRC entries at 6 AM for batches completed at 2 AM are technically within the calendar day in some interpretations, but the 2025 Enforcement Report signals MED is tightening this.

**What we're seeing on the floor (2025–2026):**
- MED inspectors arriving unannounced and requesting METRC entry timestamps against production logs — looking for gaps
- Third-party POS integrations creating entry delays of 10–30 minutes due to API sync latency — these show as "late" in the METRC audit log even when the human entry was timely
- Facilities adding a dedicated METRC compliance coordinator role specifically to manage entry timing risk

**Operator signal:** If you're running a production floor, your METRC compliance is only as strong as your shift leads' end-of-shift discipline. This is a training problem as much as a systems problem.

## 2. Pesticide Application Logs — The New Inspection Target

**The statutory expectation:** EPA-registered products only. Application log required. 48-hour re-entry interval.

**The floor reality:** The pesticide log requirement has been on the books for years but rarely prioritized in routine compliance. In 2025, that changed. MED and CDPHE began cross-checking pesticide logs against testing results — specifically looking for patterns where prohibited compounds showed up in test results but the application log showed no use.

**What we're seeing on the floor (2025–2026):**
- Inspectors requesting pesticide logs on routine inspections — not just for-cause — starting Q3 2025
- Facilities caught with supplier pesticide applications that weren't logged because the supplier's crew applied compounds before the plants were tagged as received
- The CDPHE escalation on Eagle 20EW (March 2026) has created a "search the supply chain" mentality — operators are going back to clone suppliers and asking for their pesticide histories
- IPM contractors using generic "pesticide application" entries that don't capture EPA registration numbers — inspectors flagging this as incomplete under Rule 3-1005(B)

**Operator signal:** Your pesticide log is now a primary inspection document, not a backup. Treat every IPM contractor visit like a scheduled compliance event — get their application records same day.

## 3. Camera Coverage — The Blind Spot Problem

**The statutory expectation:** Complete coverage of all required areas. 40-day retention. No gaps.

**The floor reality:** Camera systems installed under the original 2014–2016 build-out often predate the 2026 coverage requirements. Many facilities have added cultivation space, processing rooms, or retail configurations that weren't in scope when the camera system was designed. The result: technical blind spots that weren't violations under the original layout but are under the expanded 2026 requirements.

**What we're seeing on the floor (2025–2026):**
- MED inspectors conducting a "camera walk" as part of unannounced inspections — walking every camera angle to verify coverage
- The 40-day retention requirement catching facilities whose DVR systems have storage misconfiguration — footage overwrites at 30 or 35 days due to storage compression settings
- New MIP extraction rooms added via premises modification licenses not getting camera updates simultaneously — inspectors flagging this as a multi-violation (premises modification + security gap)
- The 2025 Enforcement Report category of "camera blind spots" spiked in Q4 2025, driven by facilities that expanded cultivation canopy without updating security plans

**Operator signal:** After every premises modification — even a minor one — do a camera coverage audit. Treat the security plan as a living document, not a one-time filing.

## 4. Delivery Operations — The Wild West Phase

**The statutory expectation:** Manifest before departure. Geofencing active. Badged driver. METRC transfer matched.

**The floor reality:** Delivery operator licensing is new. The MED inspection framework for delivery operations is not yet operational (expected Q3 2026). Early-mover delivery operators are essentially building compliance programs without a validated template.

**What we're seeing on the floor (2025–2026):**
- Delivery operators using consumer GPS apps as their "geofencing" solution — these are not logging platforms and will not satisfy an inspector requesting geofence compliance records
- Municipal routing restrictions intercepting deliveries mid-route — drivers not trained on which municipalities have delivery bans end up in a compliance violation that originates from a municipal, not state, requirement
- Customers changing delivery addresses after manifest creation — no clear rule on how mid-delivery address changes are documented in METRC
- Insurance carriers refusing to cover delivery vehicles — operators using personal vehicle insurance, which creates both liability and potential MED concern

**Operator signal:** Delivery is the highest-ambiguity, lowest-enforcement-history zone in Colorado cannabis right now. Build your documentation system as if every delivery will be audited. Because eventually, it will be.

## 5. Testing Lab Transitions — The Supply Chain Clock Is Running

**The statutory expectation:** All testing through ISO 17025-accredited facilities by July 1, 2026.

**The floor reality:** As of April 2026, operators have received no formal communication from MED about which labs have confirmed accreditation. Some labs have communicated directly with their operator clients; others have not. Operators with multiple product lines testing at different labs are managing accreditation uncertainty across multiple vendor relationships simultaneously.

**What we're seeing on the floor (April 2026):**
- Two Colorado testing facilities have informally confirmed accreditation timelines are on track; three have not responded to operator inquiries
- Product submitted in June 2026 that is still in testing hold status on July 1, 2026 has no clear regulatory disposition — MED has not published a transition protocol
- Operators with high-volume testing needs are pre-positioning relationships with accredited labs in anticipation of non-accredited labs losing their license
- Testing backlogs at the accredited labs are growing as the deadline approaches — turnaround times increasing

**Operator signal:** Do not wait for MED to tell you your lab is non-compliant. Verify accreditation status directly, now, with every lab you use. Identify a backup. Pre-position testing volume with your backup lab starting May 2026.

## 6. Social Equity Program — The Quiet Clock

**The statutory expectation:** Equity licensees maintain reporting obligations and program commitments throughout license term.

**The floor reality:** Many equity licensees built their compliance systems around MED's initial licensing review and haven't revisited equity program documentation since. MED has signaled through the 2025 Enforcement Report and informal industry communications that ongoing equity compliance will be scrutinized starting in 2026 renewal cycles.

**What we're seeing on the floor (2025–2026):**
- Equity licensees receiving renewal questionnaires with new equity program compliance sections — previously these were pro forma; now they're substantive
- MED requesting documentation of equity commitments (hiring, training, community investment) that many operators cannot produce because it was never systematically recorded
- Some equity licensees who transferred ownership post-licensing facing questions about whether the equity license's conditions transferred with the license

**Operator signal:** If you are an equity licensee, your equity commitments are an ongoing compliance obligation. Start documenting your equity program activities now. This is not a one-time onboarding requirement.

## 7. The Inspector Relationship — Floor Intelligence

**The statutory expectation:** Operators are subject to inspection at any time. Regulators have broad inspection authority.

**The floor reality:** The inspector-operator relationship in Colorado is highly variable by region. Denver metro inspectors operate under a more formalized protocol; rural Colorado inspections are often more conversational and less predictable. The 2025 Enforcement Report shows the highest corrective action rates in multi-facility operators — suggesting that operators managing multiple licenses across regions face compounding inspection risk.

**What we're seeing on the floor (2025–2026):**
- Unannounced inspections increasing in frequency — the 2025 Enforcement Report notes a 22% increase in unannounced visits vs. 2024
- Inspectors requesting to review METRC on a tablet or computer in real-time — operators who can't demonstrate live METRC access during an inspection are flagged
- The pre-inspection "self-audit" checklist culture is emerging among best-in-class operators — weekly internal audits using inspection-parallel checklists
- Post-inspection conversations (not on the record) often reveal what inspectors are prioritizing — these conversations are more valuable than the formal findings

**Operator signal:** Treat every week as if an inspector could walk in. The facilities that do this don't get surprised. The ones that don't are who you read about in the enforcement report.
