## Why This PENpoint Matters

Pesticide compliance is not a new obligation in Colorado cannabis. Rule 3-1005 has required EPA-registered applications and application logs since the early days of the regulated market. What changed in 2025–2026:

1. **CDPHE enforcement escalation**: Eagle 20EW (mycobutanil) findings now trigger mandatory product quarantine on first offense (CDPHE Guidance Memo, March 2026).
2. **Cross-inspection data matching**: MED and CDPHE are comparing pesticide test results against application logs — finding prohibited compounds with no documented application is a compound violation.
3. **Supply chain accountability**: The rule framework has not changed, but enforcement philosophy has shifted to include operator accountability for supplier pesticide practices.
4. **Inspection targeting**: The 2025 Enforcement Report shows a 40% year-over-year increase in pesticide-related corrective actions — the highest increase of any category.

## The Rule Framework

### Primary Obligation — Rule 3-1005 (1 CCR 212-3)

**(A) Permitted Products:**
- Only EPA-registered pesticides may be used
- Product must be labeled for cannabis, food crops, or otherwise designated non-phytotoxic
- Products on the MED/CDPHE prohibited list are disqualified regardless of EPA registration

**(B) Application Log Requirements:**
Every pesticide application must be logged with:
- Product name and EPA registration number
- Date and time of application
- Name of the applicator (individual)
- Application rate and method
- Target pest or disease being addressed
- Area/zone of application within the facility

**(C) Re-Entry Interval:**
- 48-hour minimum re-entry interval post-application in canopy areas
- Interval must be documented and access restricted during the period

**(D) IPM Plan:**
- Integrated Pest Management plan required — must be on file and available for inspector review
- IPM plan must identify approved products, approved applicators, and response protocols

## The Supply Chain Accountability Gap

The single biggest emerging enforcement exposure in Colorado pesticide compliance is **supplier accountability**. Rule 3-1005 does not contain an "innocent purchaser" provision. If prohibited compounds are found in product testing, the licensed cultivator faces the enforcement action — regardless of whether the compound was applied by the cultivator or by a clone/plant supplier.

### What We Are Seeing in 2025–2026

- Clone suppliers applying restricted-entry compounds during propagation, before plants are tagged or transferred to a licensed facility
- Third-party IPM contractors applying compounds outside the licensed premises' log system
- Wholesale flower suppliers providing product that tests positive for prohibited compounds — the downstream retailer faces action even without any direct application
- Lab testing results showing compound presence that "shouldn't be there" — leading to enforcement that traces back to supplier inputs the operator didn't control

### The Operator's Response Framework

| Risk Surface | Mitigation Action | Documentation Required |
|---|---|---|
| Clone/plant suppliers | Require supplier pesticide use disclosure at point of purchase | Supplier disclosure form, signed |
| Third-party IPM contractors | Require same-day application records from contractor | Contractor's log = your log |
| Wholesale flower inputs | Run pesticide residue testing before accepting or reselling | Pre-acceptance test COA |
| Internal application | Application log completed day-of | Your facility log under Rule 3-1005(B) |

## The Quarantine Scenario — What Happens When a Finding Occurs

With the CDPHE March 2026 escalation, a prohibited compound finding triggers the following sequence:

1. **CDPHE or MED notifies operator** of test result showing prohibited compound
2. **Mandatory product quarantine** — all affected product placed on hold in METRC; no sale, transfer, or destruction without MED authorization
3. **Remediation testing ordered** — the scope and standard of remediation testing is not defined in the rule, creating a gap in the process timeline
4. **MED corrective action** — at minimum, a corrective action plan required; potential for license suspension depending on scope and prior history
5. **Quarantine duration** — 2025 observed cases show 14 days to 6 weeks depending on MED workload and remediation test availability

**The financial impact of a quarantine event** far exceeds the cost of proactive pesticide compliance. A single quarantine affecting a full harvest batch can represent weeks of production, testing costs for remediation, and staff time for compliance response.

## Compliance Hardening Checklist

- [ ] Pesticide application log current, accessible, and meeting Rule 3-1005(B) specificity requirements  
- [ ] All compounds on premises cross-checked against current MED/CDPHE prohibited list  
- [ ] No Eagle 20EW / myclobutanil on premises or in supply chain  
- [ ] Supplier pesticide disclosure obtained for all plant and clone purchases  
- [ ] IPM contractor application records received and filed same-day  
- [ ] 48-hour re-entry intervals documented with restricted-access confirmation  
- [ ] IPM plan on file, current, reviewed at least annually  
- [ ] Pre-harvest pesticide residue testing integrated into testing protocol  
- [ ] Staff training on prohibited compounds completed and documented  
- [ ] METRC quarantine response procedure documented and staff-trained
