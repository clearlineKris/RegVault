## 1. METRC Entry Timing — The Overnight Batch Problem

### The Gap
Bulletin 26:01 (01/23/2026) reaffirms the same-business-day entry requirement. But "business day" is not defined in 1 CCR 212-3. MED facilities operating across multiple shifts — including late-night cultivation harvests or MIP production runs that conclude after 11 PM — face genuine ambiguity: does "business day" mean calendar day or operational shift?

### The Drift
The 2025 Enforcement Report (02/19/2026) shows 31% of METRC-related violations in 2025 were timing-based. MED inspectors cited overnight entries as violations in at least two publicly documented corrective action cases, even where operators demonstrated system log timestamps matching the production end time.

### The Exposure
A late-night harvest batch entered at 12:15 AM the following calendar day could be cited as a same-day violation. The risk is highest at cultivation facilities running 20-hour canopy cycles and MIPs with overnight trim shifts.

### The Posture
Document every shift's METRC entry timestamp in a parallel production log. Train shift leads to complete entries before facility close, not at the top of the next shift. If system latency is the issue, the outage reporting protocol in Bulletin 26:01 is your only documented protection.

## 2. Pesticide Enforcement — The Escalation Signal

### The Gap
Rule 3-1005 prohibits certain pesticides and requires EPA-registered applications — but the rule does not specify what "remediation testing" means, how long product quarantine can last, or what testing panel clears a quarantine hold. The CDPHE Guidance Memo (March 2026) escalated Eagle 20EW enforcement but does not define the remediation testing standard.

### The Drift
CDPHE field inspectors have begun requesting pesticide application logs on routine inspections — not just trigger-based. The 2025 Enforcement Report shows a 40% year-over-year increase in pesticide-related findings. Product quarantine periods observed in 2025 ranged from 14 days to 6 weeks depending on inspector workload.

### The Exposure
Operators who inherited grows with supplier-applied pesticides — or who use third-party IPM contractors — face exposure even without direct application knowledge. There is no "innocent purchaser" defense in the current rule text.

### The Posture
Require supplier pesticide application logs at the point of plant or clone acquisition. Build pesticide residue testing into your pre-harvest protocol, not just post-harvest. Document all IPM contractor applications the same day they occur — contractor logs do not substitute for your facility log under Rule 3-1005(B).

## 3. Canopy Cap Tracking — The METRC Divergence

### The Gap
Rule 3-215 sets canopy caps for cultivation facilities but does not define how "canopy" is measured when plants are on rolling platforms, vertical stacking systems, or multi-tier cultivation structures. MED's inspection protocols reference square footage, but modern cultivation designs create genuine measurement ambiguities.

### The Drift
MED has taken inconsistent positions on vertical canopy in site visits. One documented 2025 case treated each vertical tier as additive canopy. Another inspector treated only the footprint. The 2025 Enforcement Report notes this as an "emerging issue under review" — but no interpretive rule has been issued.

### The Exposure
A vertical cultivation operation at 1,500 sq ft footprint could be measured as 4,500 sq ft canopy if each of three tiers is counted separately. The exposure at renewal and inspection is significant for facilities operating near their licensed canopy cap.

### The Posture
Request a pre-inspection letter from MED documenting their methodology for your specific cultivation configuration. Until formal guidance issues, document your canopy measurement methodology internally and apply it consistently. Do not assume the footprint-only interpretation will hold.

## 4. Delivery Operator Geofencing — The New Frontier

### The Gap
Rule 2-260 (NEW, eff. Jan 5, 2026) requires delivery operators to implement geofencing, but the rule does not specify the technology standard, the accuracy radius required, or what a compliant geofencing system looks like. MED has not issued technical guidance.

### The Drift
This is a new rule with zero enforcement history. MED is expected to begin delivery operator inspections in Q3 2026. The absence of enforcement history means early inspections will establish the de facto standard — and the first findings will define the risk landscape for all operators.

### The Exposure
Delivery operators launching before MED's inspection framework solidifies are operating in genuine regulatory terra incognita. Technology vendors are marketing "MED-compliant" geofencing systems, but no MED certification of those systems exists.

### The Posture
Document your geofencing implementation thoroughly — system name, coverage radius, logging cadence, data retention. If you are an early-mover delivery operator, consider requesting a pre-inspection MED site visit to establish baseline compliance expectations before the formal inspection cycle begins.

## 5. ISO 17025 Testing Lab Transition — The Supply Chain Risk

### The Gap
Rule 5-110 requires all testing facilities to achieve ISO 17025 accreditation by July 1, 2026. As of April 2026, several Colorado testing facilities have not publicly confirmed accreditation timelines. The rule does not specify what happens to testing-hold product if an operator's preferred lab loses its license or fails to achieve accreditation by the deadline.

### The Drift
MED communications to the industry have been limited. No formal transition protocol has been published. Operators relying on non-accredited labs after July 1, 2026 will be unable to release product under Rule 5-125 — even product that was submitted for testing before the deadline.

### The Exposure
Operators with long-standing lab relationships may face a supply chain disruption if their lab fails the accreditation deadline. Product in the testing pipeline at a non-compliant lab on July 1 will be in a compliance grey zone.

### The Posture
Audit your testing lab relationships now. Confirm each lab's ISO 17025 accreditation status and timeline. Establish a backup lab relationship with an accredited facility. Ensure your testing submission timing does not leave active product at a non-accredited lab as the deadline approaches.

## 6. Municipal Overlay — The 300-Jurisdiction Problem

### The Gap
CRS § 44-10-104 preserves local control, allowing municipalities to ban, restrict, or independently regulate cannabis operations beyond state requirements. Colorado has over 300 municipalities, each with independent authority. The state license does not override a municipal ban or restriction.

### The Drift
Municipal enforcement has accelerated in 2025–2026, particularly around: (1) delivery operator routing restrictions, (2) consumption lounge permits, and (3) social equity applicant zoning approvals. Several municipalities have imposed de facto moratoriums through indefinite zoning review timelines.

### The Exposure
A state-licensed operator is not immune to municipal enforcement action. Municipal violations can trigger MED review of the state license. The interaction between municipal corrective action and MED license status is a documented enforcement pathway in the 2025 Enforcement Report.

### The Posture
Maintain a municipality-specific compliance calendar separate from your MED calendar. Track local ordinance changes independently — they do not flow through the MED notice process. Establish a direct relationship with your municipal licensing authority before you need it.

## 7. Visitor Log & Escort — The Paper Trail Problem

### The Gap
Rule 3-225(C) requires visitor logs, but the rule does not specify digital vs. paper, required fields beyond name/date/time, or how long logs must be retained. The 2025 Enforcement Report cited visitor log deficiencies in 23% of corrective action plans — but the deficiencies were varied: missing escort names, incomplete timestamps, and logs stored off-premises.

### The Drift
MED inspectors have been requesting visitor logs on initial unannounced visits. An incomplete log discovered on an unannounced inspection is not treated as a paperwork deficiency — it is cited as a security violation under Rule 3-225(C).

### The Exposure
Visitor logs maintained on paper by front-desk staff often have gaps during high-traffic periods. MED does not make allowances for "we were busy." A missing escort name on a single visitor entry has been cited as a standalone violation.

### The Posture
Implement a digital visitor log system with mandatory fields: visitor name, purpose, badge number issued, escort employee name, entry time, exit time. Log should auto-timestamp. Train front-desk staff that the log is a security instrument, not a hospitality courtesy.

## 8. The Social Equity Enforcement Horizon

### The Gap
Colorado's social equity licensing provisions (CRS § 44-10-202) impose reporting obligations and recapture triggers, but the statute does not define what "substantially complied" means for equity commitments, or what investigative process precedes a recapture action.

### The Drift
The 2025 Enforcement Report signals that MED is beginning to scrutinize equity license holders' ongoing compliance with equity program commitments. This is new enforcement territory — the first recapture actions are expected in 2026.

### The Exposure
Equity licensees who have not maintained required documentation of equity commitment activities face retroactive exposure as MED's equity enforcement framework matures.

### The Posture
Treat equity reporting as a primary compliance obligation, not a secondary paperwork task. Document every equity program activity contemporaneously. Consult with MED proactively if your equity program implementation has lagged behind your commitments.
