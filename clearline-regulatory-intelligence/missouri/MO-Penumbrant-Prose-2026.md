## 1. Dual-Track Transition — Medical-to-Adult-Use Boundary

### The Gap
Missouri Amendment 3 converted existing medical marijuana licensees to adult-use operators while maintaining medical license obligations in parallel. The dual-track transition requires operators to maintain separate Metrc environments, separate product labeling, and separate sales records for medical (patient verification required) and adult-use (no verification required) transactions.

### The Drift
DCR enforcement in 2023–2025 has included dual-track compliance findings — operators mixing adult-use and medical inventory in Metrc, using medical-era labels for adult-use products, and failing to verify patient status for medical transactions conducted alongside adult-use sales.

### The Exposure
Dual-track dispensaries where staff switch between adult-use (no verification) and medical (verification required) transactions without clear operational protocols are the highest-risk operations.

### The Posture
Physical or procedural segregation of adult-use and medical transaction workflows at the dispensary level. Separate POS lanes or workflow queues; staff training specific to which verification steps apply to which track.

## 2. Microbusiness Equity Licensing — Documentation Obligations

### The Gap
Missouri's Microbusiness license class prioritizes social equity applicants. Licensees who received Microbusiness licenses on equity grounds have ongoing documentation obligations — the qualification factors (e.g., prior cannabis conviction, residence in disproportionately impacted area, veteran status) must remain documentable.

### The Drift
DCR has not published detailed guidance on what happens when a Microbusiness licensee's equity qualification changes (e.g., they move out of the qualifying zip code). The ongoing documentation obligation is real but the consequence of changed circumstances is unclear.

### The Posture
Maintain all equity qualification documentation as a permanent compliance file. If circumstances change that affect your equity qualification basis, consult legal counsel before the next license renewal.

## 3. Local Authority and Opt-In Variations

### The Gap
Missouri municipalities retain authority to regulate cannabis hours, locations, and operations, and can prohibit cannabis businesses. Amendment 3 gave operators who were licensed before a municipal prohibition some grandfathering protection, but the protection is limited and the boundaries are not fully defined by the statute.

### The Drift
Several Missouri municipalities imposed restrictions after initial licensees were operating. The grandfathering protection has been tested in some markets, with inconsistent outcomes.

### The Posture
Monitor local government agendas in every operating municipality. Engage with local officials proactively. Don't assume Amendment 3 grandfathering covers all local restrictions — the scope of the protection is narrower than operators often assume.

## 4. Home Cultivation — The Six-Plant Standard

### The Gap
Amendment 3 allows home cultivation of up to 6 plants (3 mature, 3 immature) per resident adult. The statute prohibits home-grown cannabis from being sold or commercially processed. The boundary between a large personal home grow and unlicensed commercial activity is not defined by plant count alone.

### The Drift
DCR enforcement on home cultivation overlap with licensed operations is emerging. The specific risk for licensed operators: home-grown product entering the licensed supply chain (from an employee's home grow, for example).

### The Posture
Explicit SOP prohibiting any employee from bringing home-cultivated product into a licensed facility under any circumstances. Train staff on the distinction. Document the training.

---

## 5. DCR Enforcement Trajectory — Three Years In

### The Gap
Missouri's adult-use enforcement history is three years old. The DCR has moved from a launch-mode posture to active enforcement, but the pattern is still forming.

### What can be read:
- Dual-track compliance issues are the dominant enforcement category
- Packaging/labeling findings are concentrated in the first retail inspection cycle
- Metrc discrepancy reporting compliance is improving but still generating findings
- Pesticide residue findings have increased in 2025–2026 as the testing laboratory network has matured

### The Posture
Missouri operators in 2026 benefit from three years of enforcement data — more than Ohio, less than Colorado. The operators who calibrated their compliance programs to the actual enforcement record (not just the rule text) are ahead.
