## 1. The Speed Launch — Compliance Infrastructure on the Fly

**The DCR expectation:** Compliance from day one of adult-use sales.

**The floor reality:** Missouri licensed medical operators launched adult-use sales February 3, 2023, with labels, Metrc configurations, and SOPs that had been built for a medical-only environment in days, not months. The conversion speed was commercially necessary. The compliance risk was real.

**What we're seeing (2023–2026):**
- Medical-era labels appearing on adult-use shelves in the first weeks of adult-use sales — some operators hadn't had time to print new labels before the launch date
- Metrc adult-use enrollment completed but staff trained on medical-track workflows not yet retrained on adult-use-specific requirements when inspectors arrived
- DCR's first inspection cycle in 2023 was broadly understanding about launch-day gaps; the 2024–2025 cycle was not
- The "we just launched" defense has expired — 2026 DCR inspections carry full enforcement authority with no transition grace

---

## 2. Dual-Track Dispensary Operations — The Verification Pivot

**The DCR expectation:** Medical sales require patient verification; adult-use sales do not. The two tracks must be operationally distinct.

**The floor reality:** Missouri dispensaries that converted from medical-only to dual-track operations have to manage two verification workflows simultaneously. The daily operational pressure is real: a budtender helping a patient on the medical track immediately before helping an adult-use customer on the non-medical track must remember which verification step applies to which transaction.

**What we're seeing (2023–2026):**
- Dispensaries where patient verification was occasionally skipped on medical transactions during high-volume periods — DCR finding it as a "medical sales without verification" violation
- Adult-use customers being accidentally run through the medical verification workflow — operational friction, not a compliance violation, but a signal of confused protocols
- Operations using separate POS queues or lanes for medical vs. adult-use finding the split much easier to maintain accurately
- DCR inspectors arriving at dispensaries and specifically requesting the last 30 days of medical transaction records to spot-check patient registry verification

## 3. Microbusiness Operations — Floor-Level Reality

**The DCR expectation:** Microbusiness licensees operate within vertical integration limits and self-supply restrictions.

**The floor reality:** Missouri's Microbusiness set-aside created a class of small, vertically integrated operators who may not have had prior cannabis operations experience. The compliance learning curve for Microbusiness operators is steep — they are running cultivation, processing, and retail simultaneously without the institutional knowledge that larger operators have built over years.

**What we're seeing (2023–2026):**
- Microbusiness operators struggling with Metrc's complexity across three license functions (cultivate, process, retail) without dedicated compliance staff
- Scaling pressure — Microbusiness operations that are operationally successful hitting the plant count ceiling and not knowing the pathway to a Comprehensive license
- DCR compliance outreach specifically targeted at Microbusiness operators through 2024–2025 — suggesting DCR recognizes this segment has a higher baseline compliance gap
- Some Microbusiness operators using shared compliance service providers — a growing niche in the Missouri market

---

## 4. Testing Network Maturation — Pesticide Findings Increasing

**The DCR expectation:** Products tested and cleared before sale; testing records available for inspection.

**The floor reality:** Missouri's licensed testing laboratory network was relatively thin at adult-use launch in 2023. As the network has matured through 2024–2025, test result quality and coverage have improved — and pesticide residue findings have increased proportionally. More labs, more testing, more findings.

**What we're seeing (2023–2026):**
- Pesticide residue findings increasing year-over-year as the testing infrastructure has strengthened
- Supply-chain contamination (clones from uncertified suppliers, biomass from distressed operators) as the primary source of unexpected pesticide findings
- DCR treating repeat pesticide findings at the same facility as systemic violations, not isolated events
- Operators with pre-harvest testing protocols avoiding the public product quarantine that follow post-harvest positive findings

---

## 5. The 90-Day Retention Requirement — Missouri's Standard

**The DCR expectation:** 90-day camera footage retention (19 CSR 100-3.010(2)).

**The floor reality:** Missouri shares Ohio and Michigan's 90-day retention requirement — and the same infrastructure gap problem. Many Missouri dispensaries and cultivation facilities installed security systems in 2018–2020 for the medical program. Those systems were configured for 30–60 day retention. Adult-use launched without operators necessarily updating their security infrastructure first.

**What we're seeing (2023–2026):**
- DCR security reviews specifically testing retention depth — requesting footage from Day 85 as a standard inspection step
- Facilities whose systems were not updated for adult-use launch remaining non-compliant on storage capacity
- DCR including security findings in corrective action plans even when the primary inspection focus was elsewhere
