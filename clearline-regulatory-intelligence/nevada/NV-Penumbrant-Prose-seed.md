## Gray Zone 1: The Mandatory Distributor Model — Compliance at the Handoff

### The Gap
NRS 678A.285 requires all product transfers to pass through a licensed distributor. The CCB has not published clear guidance on distributor liability when a METRC discrepancy arises at the handoff between cultivator/producer and distributor. When a manifest mismatch appears, the question of which licensee bears the enforcement action is unresolved.

### The Drift
CCB enforcement actions in 2024–2025 have named both originating licensees and distributors in manifest-related findings. The distributor's acceptance of a manifest creates a de facto responsibility — but the scope of that responsibility has never been formally defined.

### The Exposure
Cultivators and producers relying on their distributor to "catch" compliance problems are exposed. The CCB's enforcement posture treats manifest discrepancies as joint violations when the distributor accepts and processes a non-conforming transfer.

### The Posture
Verify every METRC transfer record against your physical inventory before the distributor takes custody. Treat distributor acceptance as the point of no return for your compliance exposure on that batch.

## Gray Zone 2: Tourist ID Verification — The International Passport Problem

### The Gap
NRS 678A.400 requires age verification at point of sale. The statute lists acceptable ID forms but is ambiguous about: (a) international passports without a U.S.-equivalent machine-readable format, (b) foreign driver's licenses, and (c) digital ID solutions not recognized under Nevada DMV standards.

### The Drift
CCB has not issued guidance on international ID. Dispensary floor staff are making daily judgment calls on hundreds of international customers. The enforcement record shows corrective actions for "sale without adequate age verification" — but no CCB guidance on what adequate verification looks like for international visitors.

### The Exposure
A sale to an international visitor with an accepted-but-technically-noncompliant ID creates potential exposure. Given Las Vegas volumes, this is a daily operational reality, not an edge case.

### The Posture
Develop a clear, documented ID acceptance policy for international visitors. Train staff consistently. Document the policy so that an enforcement action can be met with evidence of a good-faith, systematic approach.

## Gray Zone 3: Consumption Lounge — The Legislative Promise vs. Operational Reality

### The Gap
Nevada statute permits consumption lounges, but the practical pathway to opening one is obstructed by: (1) local government approval requirements that have no defined timeline, (2) ventilation standards that are not codified in the CCB rules, and (3) liability exposure under federal law for operators in federally-assisted housing areas.

### The Drift
Several consumption lounge licenses have been issued; none are operating at scale as of 2026. The operational obstacles are structural, not technical. The penumbra here is not regulatory ambiguity — it is legislative promise without operational pathway.

### The Exposure
Operators who have built consumption lounge infrastructure in anticipation of licensing approval are carrying sunk costs against an unresolved regulatory pathway. The licensing framework exists; the enforcement framework for operating one does not.

### The Posture
Do not invest in consumption lounge build-out until the CCB publishes operational inspection standards. The license is not the product — the operational compliance standard is. Neither exists yet.

## Gray Zone 4: Retesting at the Distributor Level

### The Gap
CCB has not addressed distributor-initiated retesting. When a distributor retests a product and gets a different result from the original cultivator/producer test, there is no formal protocol for which result controls, who bears the cost, and whether the product remains in distribution hold pending resolution.

### The Drift
This is an emerging issue with minimal enforcement history. Operators and distributors report informal negotiations around retest disagreements — none of which have been validated against CCB's formal framework.

### The Exposure
A distributor-initiated retest failure on product that passed the original cultivator's testing creates a liability gap. The originating licensee may face a retroactive violation based on a test they didn't commission.

### The Posture
Include retest protocols in your distribution agreement. Define in contract which testing result controls, who bears the cost of retesting, and what dispute resolution looks like. These are not currently defined by CCB rules.

## Gray Zone 5: The Pesticide Framework Gap — No Cannabis-Specific Prohibited List

### The Gap
Unlike Colorado, Nevada does not maintain a cannabis-specific prohibited pesticide list. Operators must navigate EPA registration requirements, NDA pesticide use regulations, and CCB product safety requirements simultaneously. The absence of an explicit list creates a compliance blind spot — operators cannot "check the list" to confirm compliance.

### The Drift
CCB has cited pesticide residue findings in a number of enforcement actions without clear guidance on what operators should have done differently. The enforcement record implies a prohibited list that has never been formally published.

### The Posture
Apply Colorado's prohibited compound list as a conservative compliance baseline in Nevada. The compounds that are explicitly prohibited in Colorado (myclobutanil, imidacloprid, etc.) carry equivalent risk in Nevada even without formal NV prohibition. Treat the absence of a Nevada-specific list as a reason for more caution, not less.
