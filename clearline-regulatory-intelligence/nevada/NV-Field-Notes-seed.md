## 1. The Distributor Handoff — Where Compliance Happens or Fails

**The statutory expectation:** All product moves through a licensed distributor. METRC manifest matches physical transfer.

**The floor reality:** Nevada's mandatory distribution layer creates a compliance checkpoint that operates independently of both the cultivator/producer and the retailer. In practice, distributor pickups often happen under time pressure — cultivation facilities with harvest-ready product, distributors managing multi-stop routes, and retailers with inventory needs create a pace of transfer that strains the documentation requirements.

**What we are seeing on the NV floor:**
- Distributor drivers arriving without pre-created METRC manifests — originating licensee scrambling to create the entry before the vehicle can legally depart
- Manifest quantity discrepancies discovered at the retail receiving end — tracing back to the distributor layer but creating a retail-level compliance event
- Distributors operating as a "buffer" in the compliance chain — some operators treat distributors as if the distributor's acceptance clears their compliance exposure (it does not)
- High-volume retail receiving 15–20 distributor deliveries per day — receiving staff overwhelmed during peak inventory restocking periods

## 2. Tourist Volume — The High-Water Mark for Compliance Stress

**The statutory expectation:** Every customer verified, every transaction logged, METRC updated in real-time.

**The floor reality:** Strip dispensaries during peak tourism events (CES, EDC, major conventions, New Year's) operate at a pace that no compliance framework was designed for. Staff turnover, seasonal staffing with inconsistent training, and POS systems under load create systematic compliance stress during exactly the periods when regulatory visibility is highest.

**What we are seeing on the NV floor:**
- POS-METRC integrations experiencing sync delays during peak periods — transactions completing at the POS register but METRC confirmation lagging by minutes
- ID verification shortcuts during rush periods — staff accepting IDs they would normally flag because the line is 40 people deep
- Security camera coverage adequate for normal operations but lighting and angle issues becoming apparent during high-density floor periods
- Inventory discrepancies traced back to high-volume sales periods where receiving was rushed and METRC entries were batched rather than real-time

## 3. The Distribution Bottleneck — When Speed Meets Compliance

**The statutory expectation:** All product through licensed distributors; documentation before movement.

**The floor reality:** Nevada's mandatory distributor model creates a structural bottleneck at the point of market velocity. When a cultivator needs to move product quickly — perishable inventory, time-sensitive orders — the compliance documentation requirements can be the limiting factor. Distributors who are managing multiple accounts, multiple routes, and multiple manifest cycles simultaneously are a compliance single point of failure for multiple licensees.

**What we are seeing on the NV floor:**
- Small-scale cultivators with insufficient inventory to justify prioritized distributor attention — product sitting longer than market conditions require because distribution scheduling doesn't align with production cycles
- Distributor license concentration: a small number of licensed distributors controlling a large share of NV product movement — creating dependency and compliance leverage imbalances
- Testing holds at the distributor level creating product movement freezes that cascade across the supply chain

## 4. Cash and Banking — The Vegas Reality

**The statutory expectation:** Operator business conducted in compliance with applicable financial regulations.

**The floor reality:** Nevada's banking landscape for cannabis is marginally better than most states — Las Vegas banks have cannabis-adjacent services — but cash-heavy operations remain the reality for many operators. High-volume retail generates enormous cash inflows. Security, accounting, and regulatory compliance around that cash is a daily operational challenge.

**What we are seeing on the NV floor:**
- Cash rooms functioning as secondary compliance targets — inspectors reviewing cash handling protocols against CCB requirements
- Armored car services increasingly common for high-volume retail — but the compliance documentation around cash transport is inconsistently maintained
- Cannabis-specific banking relationships available in NV — operators who haven't accessed them are carrying unnecessary operational risk

---

## 5. Inspector Dynamics — The CCB's Active Posture

**The statutory expectation:** Operators subject to inspection; CCB authority broad.

**The floor reality:** The CCB maintains an active inspection force. Nevada operators report a higher frequency of unannounced inspections than most other states. The CCB's 2025 Annual Report reflects enforcement activity that suggests systematic inspection programs, not just reactive inspection on complaint.

**What we are seeing on the NV floor:**
- CCB inspectors conducting "document walks" on unannounced visits — requesting real-time access to METRC, visitor logs, pesticide logs, and employee badge records simultaneously
- Retail-focused inspections occurring during peak hours — testing compliance under realistic operational conditions
- Distribution-level inspections targeting the manifest/METRC documentation layer
- Post-inspection corrective action plans that require documented follow-up — operators who don't close their CAPs are tracked
