> *"Michigan was the Wild West when adult-use launched. Fastest licensing ramp, most licenses issued, lowest per-unit prices in the country by 2023. The consolidation started in 2024. What's left in 2026 are the operators who built systems, not just volume."*

## 1. Price Collapse and Compliance Pressure

**The market reality:** Michigan's oversupply problem drove wholesale flower prices below production cost for many operators in 2022–2024. Operators under margin pressure cut corners — not always intentionally. The cost-cutting that seemed invisible during boom-era growth becomes a CRA finding when the market tightens and the agency steps up enforcement.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- IPM protocol shortcuts during oversupply era surfacing as pesticide residue findings in 2025–2026 testing
- Security system maintenance deferred during cost-cutting — systems with failing cameras, missed NVR storage failures not caught until inspection
- Staff turnover at distressed operations creating Metrc training gaps — new employees entering tags incorrectly, creating discrepancy patterns
- Operators who survived consolidation sometimes absorbing acquired facilities with worse compliance histories — "we bought a problem we didn't know we had"

---

## 2. Class C Stacking Operations — The Shared Space Problem

**The CRA expectation:** Class C licensees holding multiple stacked licenses operate "separate operations."

**The floor reality:** Many Michigan operators built stacked Class C facilities with shared infrastructure — same building, same loading dock, same cold storage, same trim crew. The logic was economic (one facility is cheaper than three). The compliance risk: CRA's evolving interpretation of "separate operations" is catching up with these shared-space operations.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- CRA corrective actions citing shared harvest storage between two stacked Class C licenses as a "separate operations" violation
- Operators retrofitting shared facilities with physical barriers and separate access controls to document separation post-hoc
- Metrc discrepancies traced to shared space operations — product from one license showing up in another license's Metrc inventory
- No CRA guidance document defining "separate operations" — every enforcement action is effectively interpreting the standard for the first time

---

## 3. Metrc 24-Hour Discrepancy Rule — The Floor-Level Pressure

**The CRA rule (R 420.302(8)):** Inventory discrepancies must be reported to CRA within **24 hours** of discovery.

**The floor reality:** On a busy processing or retail shift, discovering a Metrc discrepancy at 4 PM on a Friday means the 24-hour clock runs through the weekend. Operators who file discrepancy reports Monday morning for a Friday discovery are technically late — and CRA has flagged this pattern.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Weekend discrepancy discoveries creating 24-hour compliance gaps for operators without on-call compliance coverage
- Operations trying to "resolve" discrepancies before reporting — under the belief that a resolved discrepancy doesn't need to be reported. It does.
- CRA treating late discrepancy reports as a separate compliance finding stacked on top of the underlying inventory discrepancy
- Facilities adding compliance-on-call rotations specifically to handle weekend Metrc events within the 24-hour window

---

## 4. The Municipal Opt-In Risk Surface

**The CRA rule:** Municipal opt-in is required and can change.

**The floor reality:** Michigan's local cannabis policy environment has been volatile in some municipalities. Operations that relied on informal relationships with local officials — without formal documented authorization — are the most exposed when local political winds shift.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- At least two Michigan markets where new local councils reversed cannabis-friendly policies, affecting operations that were mid-capital-investment
- Operators who invested in build-outs without formal written local authorization beyond the initial opt-in — discovering the authorization wasn't as robust as assumed
- Insurance carriers beginning to ask about municipal authorization stability as a risk factor in cannabis policy underwriting

---

## 5. Microbusiness Growth Ceiling

**The CRA rule:** Microbusiness licenses cap cultivation at 150 plants and restrict sourcing to self-supply.

**The floor reality:** Microbusiness licensees who built their operation around the Microbusiness model are hitting the growth ceiling. Moving to a standard license structure requires new applications, new capital, and navigating the Class A→B→C growth pathway.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Microbusiness operators hitting the 150-plant ceiling and attempting to informally expand — creating over-plant-count findings
- Microbusiness licensees exploring standard retailer licenses as a parallel structure to access third-party supply — CRA guidance on this is unclear (see Penumbrant Prose)
- Some Microbusiness operators choosing to stay small and compete on quality/terroir rather than scale — a viable niche strategy in Michigan's oversupplied market
