## 1. Class C License Stacking — Separate Operations vs. Shared Infrastructure

### The Gap
MCL 333.27959(3) allows a Class C grower licensee to hold up to three Class C licenses. The statute requires "separate operations" for each license. But "separate operations" is not defined — the CRA administrative rules don't specify whether separate operations requires physically separate facilities, separate entrances, separate staffing, or merely separate Metrc environments.

### The Drift
CRA enforcement activity in 2024–2025 has included stacking-related corrective actions. The enforcement language varies — some actions cite shared storage as a violation; others focus on shared Metrc entries. No consistent standard for "separate operations" has been published.

### The Exposure
Operators who built stacked Class C operations around shared infrastructure (same building, shared storage, shared staff) face the highest exposure. The question is whether the CRA draws the line at shared physical space or at shared tracking.

### The Posture
Treat separate operations as physically separate — or at minimum, formally documented with separate entrances, separate storage, and separate employee access zones. Document the separation methodology. If your operations share physical space, get a written position from CRA before inspection.

## 2. Municipal Opt-In Volatility — When the Ground Shifts Under a Licensed Operation

### The Gap
MCL 333.27956 requires municipal opt-in. Municipalities can reverse opt-in decisions or impose additional conditions on existing operations. The MRTMA does not clearly define what happens to an existing licensed operation when its municipality changes its opt-in status or imposes new restrictions after the license was issued.

### The Drift
Several Michigan municipalities opted in and subsequently added restrictions that effectively limited hours, signage, or proximity to community facilities — affecting licensed operations that predated the new restrictions. The CRA has not uniformly held that existing licensees are grandfathered against new local restrictions.

### The Exposure
Any Michigan cannabis licensee in a municipality with active local policy debates about cannabis faces the risk of retroactive local restrictions without full state-level protection.

### The Posture
Monitor local council agendas actively. Maintain documentation of your original local authorization conditions. Engage local government proactively — surprise ordinance changes are less likely when operators have a relationship with the municipality.

## 3. Microbusiness Self-Supply Restriction — The Sourcing Puzzle

### The Gap
Michigan's Microbusiness license (R 420.210) authorizes vertical integration: grow (up to 150 plants), process, and retail under one license. But Microbusiness licenses are self-supply only — the licensee cannot purchase cannabis from other licensed cultivators or processors.

What the rule doesn't clearly address: can a Microbusiness licensee also hold a separate standard retailer license and purchase product from third parties for that retail license, while self-supplying through the Microbusiness?

### The Drift
CRA has not published formal guidance on whether Microbusiness licensees can hold parallel standard retail licenses. Informally, CRA has indicated this may be permissible, but the informal guidance has been inconsistent.

### The Exposure
A Microbusiness operating as if it can source third-party product (through any mechanism) risks an unlicensed sourcing finding if CRA takes the narrow interpretation.

### The Posture
Do not source or use third-party cannabis in your Microbusiness operation without a formal written CRA determination. If you need both self-supply and third-party sourcing, structure the licenses clearly and seek CRA guidance on dual licensing before operation.

## 4. Caregiver Market Overlap — The Adjacent Unlicensed Market

### The Gap
Michigan's medical caregiver system allows registered caregivers to cultivate up to 12 plants per patient (up to 5 patients) and transfer product to those patients without a state commercial license. This caregiver market operates legally alongside — and in competition with — the licensed adult-use market.

The gray zone: when does a caregiver operation tip into unlicensed commercial activity? The MRTMA does not define this boundary in specific terms. Plant count limits exist; commercial intent is the statutory trigger.

### The Drift
CRA enforcement has pursued large-scale caregiver operations where commercial scale was evident, but the middle ground remains undefined. Operators who built businesses transitioning from caregiver networks face ongoing compliance questions about how legacy relationships must be formally restructured.

### The Posture
Any product entering a licensed facility must be sourced from a licensed facility. Caregiver relationships that predate your license do not transfer compliance status. Document your supply chain explicitly.

## 5. Temporary License "Indefinite" Operations — The Provisional Reality

### The Gap
Michigan's licensing framework included a provisional/temporary license pathway for operators who had applications pending while full licensing requirements were completed. Many operators operated on provisional licenses for extended periods — months or years — while the full license requirements were processed.

The rules governing provisional licensees are a subset of full licensee rules in some areas and the full rule set in others. The boundary is not consistently documented.

### The Exposure
Operators who received full licenses in 2024–2025 after years of provisional operation may have compliance gaps — practices that were tolerated under provisional status that don't meet full license standards.

### The Posture
If you transitioned from provisional to full license in 2024–2025, conduct a gap audit against the full R 420 rule set. Don't assume your provisional-era practices carried forward.
