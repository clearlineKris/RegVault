## 1. BioTrack in the Field — Real Compliance Friction

**The OMM expectation:** Accurate BioTrack tracking from propagation through sale.

**The floor reality:** Pennsylvania's BioTrack-based tracking environment is genuinely different from Metrc. Staff who have worked in Metrc states approach Pennsylvania operations with muscle memory that doesn't apply. The result: BioTrack entries that look correct to a Metrc-trained eye are structurally wrong in BioTrack's lot-based framework.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Grower/Processors creating harvest entries in BioTrack using Metrc-style individual package logic rather than BioTrack's lot-based structure — creating reconciliation failures on inspection
- Transfer manifests created against the wrong BioTrack lot — product arriving at a dispensary with manifest records that don't match the lot number in BioTrack
- Dispensary staff entering sales against the wrong BioTrack lot — an error that compounds over time into large inventory discrepancies
- Multi-state operators entering Pennsylvania and systematically underestimating the BioTrack retraining required — treating it as a "same as Metrc but different name" transition

## 2. The Pharmacist Presence Compliance Gap

**The OMM expectation:** Pharmacist involved in dispensary operations; available for patient consultation.

**The floor reality:** Pennsylvania's pharmacist requirement creates a real operational cost. Board-certified pharmacists available for cannabis dispensary work are not a large population. Operators in rural markets or operators running multiple locations face the practical challenge of pharmacist coverage.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Some dispensary organizations using a pharmacist-on-call / telehealth consultation model — OMM has informally accepted this in some cases but not universally
- Pharmacists who are on the license application but not functionally integrated into operations — discovered on OMM inspections when inspectors ask to speak with the pharmacist and none is available or reachable
- Higher pharmacist salary premiums in Pennsylvania cannabis than in medical settings, reflecting the specific demand
- Operators investing in pharmacist partnership arrangements — pharmacists who serve multiple dispensary organizations as compliance partners rather than employees

## 3. 90-Day Retention — Pennsylvania's Standard

**The OMM expectation:** 90-day camera footage retention across all licensed locations.

**The floor reality:** Pennsylvania medical marijuana operators who have been licensed since 2019–2020 have security systems that are now 5–7 years old. The same storage capacity and hardware aging problem documented in Michigan and Missouri applies here. Pennsylvania's multi-location dispensary structure adds a compounding factor: the problem multiplies by the number of locations.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Multi-location operators discovering that one location's NVR is compliant and two others are not, following a portfolio security audit
- OMM inspectors requesting footage from a specific date 80+ days prior as a standard retention test — operators who assumed "90 days" was covered without testing it fail this test
- Hardware refresh cycles being added to annual compliance calendars for operators who've recognized the aging infrastructure risk

---

## 4. Multi-Location Compliance Equity — The Lowest-Attention Location Risk

**The OMM expectation:** Every licensed location compliant independently.

**The floor reality:** Multi-location dispensary organizations naturally develop location hierarchies in terms of management attention. The flagship location gets the most attention; the third location is often the compliance problem waiting to happen.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- OMM audit cycles that begin with one location and expand to portfolio reviews when findings emerge — triggering inspections at locations that had not been recently audited
- Staff turnover at lower-attention locations leading to BioTrack entry drift — errors that accumulate over months without correction because compliance oversight is thin
- Operators implementing quarterly internal compliance audits at all locations (not just the flagship) as a response to OMM portfolio-level inspection expansion

---

## 5. Adult-Use Anticipation Compliance Strategy

**The OMM expectation:** Medical compliance only, currently.

**The floor reality:** Pennsylvania medical operators who believe adult-use legalization is coming are making compliance investments now that position them for the transition. The operators who are building to adult-use standards today are ahead of the curve — and are also building more defensible current compliance programs.

**What we're seeing (2024–2026):**
- Major Pennsylvania medical operators investing in adult-use-grade security infrastructure (90-day retention at high camera counts, modern NVR systems)
- Grower/Processors moving from BioTrack-only training to dual-system training — learning Metrc in anticipation of a transition
- Packaging programs being designed with dual-track flexibility — medical labeling compliant today with adult-use labeling ready to deploy without a full redesign
