## Section 1: Licensing Framework

### 1.1 Statutory Foundation

- **Minn. Stat. § 342.01 et seq.** establishes OCM authority, license categories, and operator obligations  
- **Minn. Stat. § 342.17** establishes social equity applicant criteria and program obligations  
- **Minn. Stat. §§ 342.28–342.38** define primary adult-use license categories and operational boundaries  
- **Minn. Stat. § 342.13** preserves local control and municipal retail opt-out authority  

### 1.2 Minnesota License Categories — 2026 Operative Landscape

| License Type | Statutory Anchor | 2026 Operating Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Cannabis Cultivator | § 342.28 | Active licensing and staged operational onboarding |
| Cannabis Manufacturer | § 342.30 | Active; production controls under evolving OCM guidance |
| Cannabis Retailer | § 342.32 | Active; municipal status is primary siting gate |
| Cannabis Delivery Service | § 342.33 | Developing; implementation guidance still maturing |
| Cannabis Wholesaler | § 342.34 | Active with transfer documentation scrutiny |
| Cannabis Event Organizer | § 342.35 | Conditional and locality-sensitive |
| Cannabis Transporter | § 342.36 | Active; chain-of-custody documentation emphasized |
| Cannabis Testing Facility | § 342.38 | Active with accreditation and readiness focus |
| Microbusiness / Mezzobusiness | § 342.40+ | Core equity and small-operator access pathway |
| Lower-Potency Hemp Retailer | § 342.46 | Active under overlapping agency oversight |

### 1.3 Licensing Compliance Checklist

- [ ] Current OCM license and local authorization maintained and posted where required  
- [ ] Ownership and beneficial ownership disclosures current and complete  
- [ ] Background checks and principal officer documentation current for renewals  
- [ ] Financial source documentation updated for new capital injections  
- [ ] Insurance and risk coverage aligned with OCM requirements  
- [ ] License-premises scope matches actual operations and floor layout

## Section 2: Social Equity & Program Integrity

### 2.1 Equity Framework Core Requirements

Minnesota's equity design is not a one-time licensing gate. It is an ongoing compliance framework tied to reporting, operational posture, and program integrity over the life of the license.

**Key compliance elements:**
- Eligibility qualification and retention documentation  
- Ongoing reporting obligations to OCM  
- Transfer and restructuring scrutiny where equity commitments may be diluted  
- Potential recapture exposure where commitments are not maintained  

### 2.2 Equity Program Documentation Checklist

- [ ] Equity qualification evidence retained and annually validated internally  
- [ ] Equity program activity log maintained contemporaneously  
- [ ] Workforce, training, and community investment records organized for inspection  
- [ ] Counsel-reviewed file prepared for any ownership restructuring event  
- [ ] Internal quarterly equity compliance review completed and archived

## Section 3: Municipal Control & Local Siting

### 3.1 Local Control Baseline

Under **Minn. Stat. § 342.13**, municipalities can prohibit retail operations and shape local land-use posture. State licensure does not neutralize municipal prohibitions.

### 3.2 Operator Duties in a Dynamic Municipal Map

- Confirm municipal opt-in / opt-out status before committing capital  
- Track ordinance calendars, hearings, and policy reversals in active markets  
- Align site strategy with zoning realities and local process timelines  
- Treat each municipality as a distinct compliance environment  

### 3.3 Municipal Readiness Checklist

- [ ] Municipality status verified before LOI/lease execution  
- [ ] Local counsel or municipal specialist assigned for each active target market  
- [ ] Ordinance-change monitoring cadence established (weekly or biweekly)  
- [ ] Contingency plan documented for municipal posture reversal

## Section 4: Seed-to-Sale Tracking & Inventory Controls

### 4.1 Tracking Architecture

Minnesota is implementing OCM-governed tracking while managing legacy system transition for medical operators. This creates a heightened documentation burden during 2025–2026.

### 4.2 Core Tracking Obligations

| Obligation | Expected Control | Execution Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Plant-level tagging | Unique identifier at early lifecycle stage | Same-day capture and verification |
| Batch creation | Formal harvest/production lot creation | Complete and timestamped |
| Transfers | Pre-movement documentation | Reconciled with receiving records |
| Reconciliation | Inventory count vs system data | Scheduled and discrepancy-logged |
| Testing hold | Product segregation pending results | No release before authorized status |

### 4.3 Dual-System Transition Controls (Legacy Medical Operators)

- Maintain crosswalk documentation between legacy records and OCM system entries  
- Log every discrepancy with root cause and corrective action  
- Preserve transition communications and guidance records from OCM  
- Conduct higher-frequency self-audits during onboarding windows  

### 4.4 Tracking Checklist

- [ ] Tracking roles assigned by shift and backed by SOP  
- [ ] Weekly reconciliation documented and management-reviewed  
- [ ] Discrepancy log maintained with closure dates and owner assignments  
- [ ] System outage/workaround process documented and auditable

## Section 5: Security, Surveillance & Access

### 5.1 Security Baseline

OCM rules require robust surveillance, access control, alarm coverage, and visitor governance for all licensed premises where cannabis is present.

### 5.2 Security Control Matrix

| Control Domain | Minimum Expectation | Risk if Weak |
|---|---|---|
| Camera coverage | Complete coverage of cannabis-handling zones | Inspection findings and evidentiary gaps |
| Retention | Rule-compliant retention windows | Inability to validate events post-incident |
| Access badging | Role-based access and credential control | Unauthorized access exposure |
| Visitor controls | Log + escort protocol | Security and diversion concern |
| Alarm & monitoring | Active monitored system | Incident-response and compliance risk |

### 5.3 Security Checklist

- [ ] Camera map current with facility layout and blind-spot checks  
- [ ] Retention settings validated against OCM minimums  
- [ ] Visitor log standardized and retained per policy  
- [ ] Badge issuance/revocation controls documented  
- [ ] Annual internal security audit completed and archived

## Section 6: Testing, Product Holds & Release Controls

### 6.1 Testing Baseline

Minnesota requires licensed-facility testing prior to sale. At a minimum, operators should expect potency, contamination, and safety panels aligned with OCM directives.

### 6.2 Required Operator Controls

- Chain-of-custody integrity from sampling through result receipt  
- Testing hold segregation until authorized release  
- Failed test response protocol with disposition controls  
- Label/COA consistency check before distribution  

### 6.3 Testing Checklist

- [ ] Testing submission SOP current and staff-trained  
- [ ] Hold inventory physically and digitally segregated  
- [ ] Failed-lot investigation protocol documented and used  
- [ ] COA archive maintained and cross-referenced to released batches  

---

## Section 7: Transportation & Transfer Compliance

### 7.1 Transfer Integrity Baseline

Transport and wholesale activity carry heightened chain-of-custody exposure in emerging markets where process variance is common.

### 7.2 Transfer Checklist

- [ ] Pre-departure manifests complete and accurate  
- [ ] Vehicle and driver controls aligned with OCM requirements  
- [ ] Receiving confirmation process active for every transfer  
- [ ] Variance/escalation SOP active for delayed or failed transfers

## Section 8: Lower-Potency Hemp (LPH) Boundary Management

Minnesota's LPH category creates a parallel commerce channel with overlapping consumer pathways and dual-agency implications.

### 8.1 Boundary Risk Controls

- Potency testing rigor at intake and pre-sale stages  
- SKU governance preventing cross-category confusion  
- Training for staff communicating LPH vs adult-use distinctions  
- Clear enforcement-response plan for out-of-bound potency events

## Section 9: 2026 Rule Transition Operating Posture

### 9.1 Practical Posture for Operators

- Build controls to expected final standards, not interim minimums  
- Preserve written regulator communications and policy updates centrally  
- Run monthly compliance delta reviews as OCM guidance evolves  
- Assign one owner for rule-transition execution accountability  

### 9.2 Executive-Level Control Questions

- Where are we currently over-indexed on interim assumptions?  
- Which controls would fail first under stricter final rules?  
- Do we have enough evidence quality to survive a hard audit cycle?
