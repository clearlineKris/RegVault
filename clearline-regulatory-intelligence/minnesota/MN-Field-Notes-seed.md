## 1. The First Wave — Operating While the Rules Are Still Coming

**The statutory expectation:** OCM licenses operators and establishes requirements. Operators comply.

**The floor reality:** Minnesota operators who received their licenses in the first issuance waves are operating against rules that OCM was still finalizing when they opened. Interim guidance is the operative standard for some requirements. Some operators received conflicting guidance from OCM staff on the same question.

**What we are seeing on the MN floor (2025–2026):**
- Operators building standard operating procedures against interim OCM guidance, then having to revise when final rules differ
- OCM inspectors visiting first-wave operators before formal inspection protocols were fully developed — inspections that were simultaneously compliance checks and OCM learning experiences
- Operators who engaged OCM early and proactively building better compliance programs than those who waited for finalized rules
- The industry association community in Minnesota is unusually active — operators sharing notes more openly than in mature markets because everyone is navigating the same uncertainty

## 2. The Equity License Experience — First Year on the Floor

**The statutory expectation:** Equity licensees hold valid licenses; maintain equity program commitments; report to OCM.

**The floor reality:** Minnesota's equity licensing program is in its first year of real-world operation. Equity operators are navigating: lower capitalization (by design, as a barrier-reduction measure), an OCM that is still developing its equity oversight framework, and a market where established operators have significant capital and operational advantages.

**What we are seeing on the MN floor (2025–2026):**
- Equity operators facing cash flow challenges in the early market — the equity license reduced the barrier to entry but didn't eliminate operational cost pressures
- OCM equity program staff conducting check-in visits that blend compliance review with support — the early OCM equity relationship is notably more collaborative than enforcement in mature markets
- Equity operators with strong community roots outperforming expectations on customer loyalty and local market penetration — the equity program is producing operators who understand their communities
- Documentation of equity program activities is inconsistent across the equity licensee population — some operators have robust records; others have almost nothing

## 3. The Municipal Landscape — Operating Across 300+ Different Regulatory Environments

**The statutory expectation:** State law governs operational requirements; municipalities can opt out.

**The floor reality:** For operators in Minnesota's metro areas, the municipal landscape is reasonably predictable. For operators considering expansion outside the metro, or operating in communities where opt-out sentiment is present, the municipal layer is an active operational variable.

**What we are seeing on the MN floor (2025–2026):**
- Operators in Greater Minnesota (outside the Twin Cities metro) facing significant municipal uncertainty — communities that opted in during the initial window are revisiting those decisions
- Municipal ordinances imposing local zoning and land use requirements that, while nominally consistent with state law, are effectively restrictive through process
- Operators in tribal and reservation-adjacent areas navigating a three-layer jurisdictional environment (state, local, tribal) that has no clear compliance framework
- The Minneapolis/St. Paul metro has lower opt-out risk than outstate Minnesota — operators building metro-first expansion strategies are explicitly managing municipal risk

## 4. The Tracking System Transition — Legacy Medical Operators on the Floor

**The statutory expectation:** All operators use the OCM tracking system; records are accurate and timely.

**The floor reality:** Former medical cannabis operators transitioning to OCM oversight are living with two compliance systems simultaneously. The legacy Minnesota Medical Cannabis registry and the new OCM system have different structures, different entry requirements, and different data fields. Staff trained on the legacy system require retraining.

**What we are seeing on the MN floor (2025–2026):**
- Legacy operators experiencing data export/import issues when attempting to migrate historical records to the new OCM system — records that were fully compliant under the legacy system don't map cleanly to OCM data structure
- OCM inspectors visiting legacy operators and finding the new system onboarding incomplete — inspections occurring during a transition window where neither system is fully authoritative
- New-to-cannabis operators (equity licensees and others without legacy program history) having a simpler onboarding experience than legacy operators — they're building on a clean slate

## 5. The LPH Market — A Market Parallel to Cannabis

**The statutory expectation:** Lower-potency hemp products meet defined potency thresholds; OCM and MDA jurisdiction applies.

**The floor reality:** Minnesota's LPH market is a parallel cannabis-adjacent market with its own operators, retail channels, and compliance requirements. The lines between LPH operators and cannabis operators are blurring as both sectors mature. Some operators hold both LPH and cannabis licenses — managing dual regulatory obligations simultaneously.

**What we are seeing on the MN floor (2025–2026):**
- LPH retail at liquor stores, convenience stores, and other non-cannabis-licensed locations — a distribution channel that does not exist for adult-use cannabis
- Consumer confusion between LPH and adult-use cannabis products — cannabis-licensed retailers encountering customers who have been purchasing LPH products elsewhere and expecting the same experience
- MDA inspections of LPH retailers occurring with different protocols than OCM cannabis inspections — operators holding both license types navigating two inspection frameworks
